Why should you switch to using Menstrual Cups instead of Pads/Tampons?

Before we get into it, if you are new to the idea of menstrual cups, and find it difficult to get accustomed to from the looks of it, I feel you. I have been there and I’m sure most of the women who made the switch have been there too.
Just remember, it is not as uneasy and difficult as it may appear.

I have tried to look at the aspects of using Menstrual Cups from both ‘HYGIENE’ and ‘ENVIRONMENTAL’ points of view.
Find your reasons, but make the switch for whichever one sounds more convincing to you.

The Menstrual Hygiene Alliance of India (MHAI) has approximated 121 million women and adolescent girls in India use disposable sanitary napkins. An individual goes through approximately 11,000 disposable pads and/or tampons in a lifetime.
Now, hold tight.

Depending on the materials used in the manufacture of the sanitary pads, it could take up to 800 years to decompose a single sanitary napkin. 800 YEARS. FOR A SINGLE SANITARY NAPKIN. Multiply that number by everyone on this planet that gets her period and that equals a substantial amount of waste.

Hygienic Aspects-

While the actual products contribute largely to the problem, we should also take the life-cycle of the products into consideration.

First of all, we have the raw material extraction, which involves the production of cotton (a very water intensive process; requiring six pints of water to grow just one little bud). Most use non-organic cotton, which has been saturated in pesticides and insecticides.

Secondly, most pads contain polyethylene plastic (the adhesive that’s used to make the pad stick to your underwear), which is an environmentally harmful pollutant. While the products sit in landfills, these chemicals get soaked up by the earth and are released as pollution into groundwater and air.

Doesn’t seem like any of the above substances should get anywhere near the most sensitive area on our body.

Environmental Aspects-

Tampons, pads s along with their packaging and individual wrapping generate more than 200,000 tonnes of waste per year, and they all contain plastic – in fact, pads are around 90% plastic!
The average user throws away an astonishing 125 to 150kg of tampons, pads in their lifetime.

Plastic waste ends up in a landfill or, even worse in the oceans, rivers and beaches.
The time it takes for a tampon or pad to degrade in a landfill is centuries longer than the lifespan of the individual who used it – particularly when it’s wrapped in plastic.
The lifespan of the cup means potentially saving the environment for the packaging and material waste of more than 2400 pads or tampons per user.

Menstrual Cup-

A menstrual cup is a flexible cup made of medical-grade silicone or rubber which sits in the vagina and collects blood unlike cloth, sanitary pads and tampons which absorb blood. Some cups are long-lasting and reusable, while others are disposable.

Silicone is a pretty green ingredient. The material is derived from silica, a type of sand, and as it degrades, it will slowly go back to its original state; the second most abundant mineral in the Earth’s crust and one that isn’t hazardous to the environment.

Clearly, a cleaner and a greener alternative!
